# What ChatGPT's Goblin Obsession Can Tell You About AIs

*May 1, 2026*

I describe my AI like a quirky, very literal employee. Brilliant. Tireless. Occasionally fixated on something weird.

Here's a true story of the ridiculousness.

Starting with GPT-5.1 last November, ChatGPT began dropping goblins, gremlins, trolls, and pigeons into otherwise normal work. Goblin mentions jumped 175%. By GPT-5.5, OpenAI had to put what amounts to a sticky note on the model's monitor in Codex: *"stop talking about goblins."*

The cause was almost too on-the-nose. A "Nerdy" personality option carried just 2.5% of responses — but accounted for 67% of all goblin mentions. The reward signal meant to make Nerdy playful was rewarding creature metaphors. Those outputs got recycled into the next round of training. The goblins spread.

If you've ever managed people, this tracks. Your most talented employee has a tic. What makes them great in one mode bleeds into every mode.

## You don't have to wait for the next training run

Here's the part most execs miss: just like an employee, you can tell it to stop.

Example. Claude can't actually tell time. But it kept telling me to "call it a day" at 11AM and reminding me to get some rest at 9PM. After the tenth nag in a single day, I yelled "F-off, I'm an adult" at my laptop — at which point my wife walked in and asked if I needed to take a break. I apologized to my wife and our Goldendoodle, then wrote instructions telling Claude to stop annoying me (in that way).

That's what skills are for. That's what a doctrine document does. The hardcoded override OpenAI bolted on as duct tape is the governance layer you should be building anyway. Not as a patch. As a system.

If you're running AI in production without a written set of rules it has to follow, you're managing a quirky employee with no job description.

Charming bug. Real lesson.
