# Fixing Biased Leadership Systems with Data: Male Allyship in Action

*Published in The Table · March 2, 2026*

*After being invited to write about inclusive leadership, Dr. Ed Dobbles, DBA did what he's done for 25 years in analytics — he turned to the data. What he found challenged long-held assumptions about potential ratings, performance reviews, non-promotable work, and leadership effectiveness.*

*Ed shares a candid reflection with The Table community on what male allyship really requires, exploring how leaders can apply the same rigor to allyship that they apply to business performance. If your leadership model produces biased outcomes, the model — not the people — needs fixing.*

---

A few months ago, Charley May asked me to write about inclusive leadership.

Charley runs The Table — a community built around making sure every female leader has a seat — and she'd invited me in as a member. I was honored. And then I froze. Because how do you write about the working woman's experience when you've never lived it? I'm not going to mansplain something I don't understand. But I also didn't want to just decline and go back to being comfortably clueless.

So I started by listening. More deliberately than I had before.

I'm surrounded by women I respect — my wife, my daughter, close friends — and I've always heard about their days over dinner. But after Charley's ask, I started paying closer attention to the frustrations they bring home from work.

Everyone has work frustrations. I've got my own. But the more I listened, the more I noticed that some of theirs have a different texture than mine. The client who talks over a woman to address her male colleague. Getting volunteered for things nobody else wants to do. Feedback that's more about personality than performance. Some of the people they describe sound like jerks. Some of them are probably just clueless. And at some point, a question crept in that I couldn't shake: which one am I? Not the jerk — I'm pretty confident there. But clueless? That one I couldn't rule out.

I've been leading analytics teams for 25 years. I've managed women, promoted women, mentored women. But hearing what these women were telling me — really hearing it this time — I started wondering whether I've got blind spots I don't even know about. Whether some of the stuff they're describing happened on my watch, and I just couldn't see it from where I sat. That bugged me. Because I'm a data guy. It's literally what I do. I've spent my career telling Fortune 500 execs that their gut instincts are wrong and here's the proof. I've stared down 18 million rows of venue data and found the story hiding in it. I once eliminated 38% of a database because the duplicates were hiding the real story. But I'd never once turned that lens on my own leadership. So I did what I always do. I pulled the research. Not the think pieces or the hot takes — the actual peer-reviewed, large-sample studies. My Rutgers DBA program taught me how to read academic research, and I'll admit that's not a skill I expected to use at the dinner table. But here we are.

I want to be honest about something: most women already know everything I'm about to share. They don't need a meta-analysis to confirm what they've lived. This article isn't for them. It's for the guys like me who needed to see it in the data before they believed it.

What I found changed how I think about teams. Not just the ones I've led for the past 25 years, but the next one I'm going to build. It changed things not because it told me what women experience — I still don't know that, and I'm not going to pretend I do. It changed things because it showed me where systems like mine produce biased outputs. And that's a problem I know how to work.

## Here's what I wasn't asking...

When I was leading teams, I never once walked up to a woman on my staff and said, "So, what sucks about being a woman in the workforce?" That's not a conversation. That's an ambush. And it puts someone in the position of educating the boss about their own marginalization — which is its own kind of unpaid work.

But I also couldn't just look back on 25 years of leadership and assume that treating everyone the same meant everyone got the same experience. The performance data across industries says that's not true. So the question I landed on wasn't "what was it like for them." It was: "where were my processes producing different outcomes for different people — and did I even know?"

## 1. The finding that stopped me cold

Researchers at Yale and MIT got access to performance data from 30,000 managers at a major company. [1] If you've been in leadership, you know the Nine Box grid — that matrix every HR department uses to decide who gets developed, who gets fast-tracked, who gets the next promotion. One axis is performance. The other is potential.

The performance axis looked fine. Women received higher ratings than men. Consistently. Across the board. But the potential axis was where the leak was happening.

Women received lower potential ratings. And here's the part that should make every analytics leader uncomfortable: the potential ratings were wrong. The women rated as "lower potential" went on to outperform the men who were rated higher. The system wasn't just unfair. It was inaccurate. It was producing bad predictions.

If I found a predictive model in my business that was systematically wrong for 50% of the population, I wouldn't call that a diversity issue. I'd call it a broken model. And I'd fix it before lunch.

## 2. The 200-hour tax you're not tracking

Here's one that hit close to home. Researchers at the University of Pittsburgh studied how non-promotable tasks get distributed in organizations. [2] The stuff that has to get done but doesn't show up in anyone's performance review — organizing the team lunch, taking notes in the meeting, onboarding the new hire, sitting on the committee nobody wants to join.

In mixed-gender groups, women volunteered for these tasks about 50% more than men. But it wasn't just volunteering. Women were asked more often. And when asked, they said yes 76% of the time. Men? 51%.

The researchers estimated that in one professional services firm, this added up to roughly 200 extra hours per year of work that didn't advance anyone's career. Two hundred hours. That's five weeks of full-time work spent on stuff that will never show up in a promotion conversation.

I thought about my own team when I read that. And I didn't have to think very hard. There's a woman in my broader organization — Jackie — who I asked to be the onboarding buddy for nearly every new employee I hired. Not once or twice. Repeatedly. She even made fun of me once for always being the one to ask her. I laughed it off. Didn't think about it. I'm thinking about it now. Sorry, Jackie. She was great at it, which is exactly why I kept asking. But "she's great at it" is how this pattern perpetuates itself.

The person who's good at the thankless work gets more of the thankless work, and it's not a coincidence that "the person" is usually a woman.

The fix isn't complicated. Rotate the assignments. Track who does what. Stop asking for volunteers — because the research is clear that "who volunteers?" is really just a proxy for "who feels the most social pressure to say yes?" Build a rotation and make it visible. It's not a culture initiative. It's workload management.

## 3. Your reviews are saying different things to different people

A study out of Stanford got access to written performance reviews at a Fortune 500 tech company and coded the language managers used. [3] What they found should concern anyone who's ever written or received a review.

Men and women were described as equally technically capable. No gap there. But "taking charge" — the kind of initiative that drives top ratings — was strongly associated with high scores for men and had almost no effect on women's scores. Same behavior, different outcome. It gets worse. Women were significantly more likely to receive feedback about their personality rather than their performance.

A separate industry survey of 25,000 people found women were 22% more likely to get personality-based feedback and eleven times more likely to be described as "abrasive." [4] Think about that for a second. If I give one employee feedback on what they delivered and another employee feedback on who they are, I haven't given them the same review. I've given one person a roadmap and the other person a personality critique. And the data says that split isn't random — it tracks with gender.

The thing is, most managers don't realize they're doing this. I'm sure the managers in that study thought they were being fair. The bias lives in the language, not the intent. So what do you do about it? Anchor your reviews to specific behaviors and outcomes. "You led the client migration and it came in under budget" is useful. "You need to work on your presence" is not — and it's the kind of vague feedback that disproportionately lands on women.

If you run a team, audit your own review language. If you run an organization, audit it systematically. The tools exist to do this at scale now.

## 4. The leadership effectiveness gap that isn't

I saved this one because it reframes everything above.

A meta-analysis aggregated decades of research on gender and leadership effectiveness. [5] Hundreds of studies. Over 100,000 data points. And the finding is clear: there is no meaningful gender difference in leadership effectiveness. If anything, women are rated slightly more effective by everyone except themselves. Men rate themselves higher, but external evaluations — peers, subordinates, superiors — give women a slight edge.

Women also score higher on transformational leadership — the style most consistently linked to team performance. [6] They're rated higher on developing others, on ethical leadership, on building trust.

But here's the part that makes the pattern so frustrating: despite being rated as equally or more effective, women are less likely to be selected for leadership. They emerge less often in unstructured group settings. [7] They get lower "potential" scores. They hit the broken rung at the first promotion to manager — McKinsey's data shows that for every 100 men promoted to that first managerial role, only 81 women make the same step. [8]

So we have a system that under-selects people who, by its own measurements, perform as well or better once selected. If that doesn't bother you as a data person, I don't know what would.

## What I'm doing differently next time

I'm not going to wrap this up with a manifesto. I'm a guy who looked at some data during a career transition and realized his systems needed work. Here's what changes with my next team:

**I'm validating potential assessments against reality.** If I rate someone as high-potential, I'm going to check that against their actual trajectory. If I'm consistently wrong about one group, the model needs recalibrating — not the people.

**I'm building a tool to keep me honest.** We all have transcriptions from our Teams, Google, and Zoom meetings now. We're sitting on a mountain of data about how we actually lead — who we ask to do what, how we give feedback, whose ideas we amplify, whose we talk over. I'm building an AI tool that tracks my own language and assignment patterns. Not because I think I'm a bad manager. Because I know I have blind spots, and I'd rather have a machine flag them than find out the hard way.

**I'm auditing my feedback language from day one.** When I write a review, I'm going to ask myself: am I talking about what this person did, or who this person is? Am I giving actionable guidance or a personality assessment? The AI tool helps here too — if I'm consistently giving one group behavior-based feedback and another group personality-based feedback, I want to see that pattern before my team does.

**I'm not waiting for someone to tell me there's a problem.** The whole point of data is that you don't have to wait for the complaint. You can see the pattern before it becomes a crisis. That's how I've run every analytics function I've ever built. It's time I applied it to leadership itself.

**I'm putting myself in rooms where I'm not the expert.** There's a reason I joined The Table. The research can show you the patterns. Being in the room with people who've lived those patterns is a different kind of education. I'm not there to talk. I'm there to listen.

---

Here's where I've landed, and it's not where I expected.

This isn't really about being a jerk or being clueless. I started there — "which one am I?" — but that's the wrong frame. The real question is whether I'm good at my job.

If I'm burning 200 hours of someone's year on work that doesn't develop them, that's not a character flaw. That's a resource allocation failure. If my potential ratings don't predict actual performance, that's not a sensitivity problem. That's a broken model. If my review language gives one group a roadmap and another group a personality critique, I'm not developing my talent evenly. I'm developing some of it and wasting the rest.

That's not clueless. That's bad management. And in the long run, bad management catches up with you — whether it's attrition, disengagement, or just a team that never hits its ceiling because half the people on it are running uphill with a weighted vest you didn't know you handed them.

The research doesn't ask you to feel guilty. It asks you to look at your data. That's a much easier ask for people like us. So look at it.

I still don't know what it's like to be a working woman. But I know where my systems were leaking. And now I know how to find the leaks in yours. That's a start.
